Urgency to take action: Phishing emails often create a sense of urgency, trying to prompt the recipient into taking some sort of action (e.g., "Your account will be closed if you don't update your password within the next hour"). 2. Suspicious links or attachments: Phishing emails may contain links that seem legitimate but actually lead to malicious websites or attachments that can download malware. 3. Generic greetings: Phishing emails often use generic greetings like "Dear user" instead of addressing you by your actual name. 6. Overly formal language: Scammers may try to sound overly formal or official in their emails, which can raise suspicions. 7. Threats or warnings: Phishing emails might claim that your account has been compromised or that you need to take immediate action to avoid consequences. It's essential to be cautious and verify the authenticity of an email before taking any action. If you're unsure about a particular email, it's always best to err on the side of caution and report it as suspicious.

Query 3: What was the Google Docs phishing attack?

Ground Truth: A 2017 attack where victims received emails appearing from contacts sharing Google Docs. When authorized, a third-party app harvested contacts and forwarded to others, affecting about one million Gmail users.

Response Time: 4.06s | Relevance Score: 0.54

The Google Docs phishing attack, also known as the "Google Docs Worm," was a sophisticated email-based phishing attack that sent victims an email claiming someone they knew had shared a Google Doc. When users clicked the link and authorized what appeared to be Google Docs, they actually gave access to a third-party app named "Google Docs" which then harvested their contacts and forwarded the phishing email to everyone in their address book. This attack affected approximately one million Gmail users before Google shut it down within an hour.
Query 4: What is spear phishing and how does it differ from regular phishing?

Ground Truth: Spear phishing targets specific individuals or organizations, unlike regular phishing which casts a wide net with generic messages to many potential victims.

Response Time: 6.40s | Relevance Score: 0.57

Spear phishing is a type of phishing attack that targets specific individuals or organizations, often based on prior knowledge of the victim's identity or characteristics. This differs from regular phishing attacks, which typically use broad, unsolicited emails to catch unsuspecting victims. In spear phishing, attackers usually conduct thorough research on their target, gathering information about their interests, job title, company, or other identifying factors. This helps them craft a more convincing and personalized message that appears to come from someone the victim trusts. The goal is often to trick the individual into divulging sensitive information or performing an action that benefits the attacker. Spear phishing attacks are often more sophisticated and targeted than regular phishing attempts, making them more difficult to detect and respond to. They can be particularly effective because they tap into the human tendency to trust familiar names and faces, making it easier for attackers to manipulate victims into taking the bait.

Response Time: 7.99s | Relevance Score: 0.36

Based on the context, specifically the Real-world phishing example: The Gmail/Google Docs Worm (2017), I can identify some common techniques used by phishers to create a sense of urgency: 1. **Time-sensitivity**: Phishers might claim that the victim needs to take immediate action, such as updating their account information or verifying their identity, within a specific time frame. 2. **Limited-time offers**: Attackers might pretend to offer exclusive deals, promotions, or limited-time opportunities to create a sense of FOMO (fear of missing out). 3. **Sense of responsibility**: Phishers may try to make the victim feel responsible for taking action quickly, such as by claiming that their account has been compromised or that there's an issue with their personal information. 4. **Fear-mongering**: Attackers might exploit fear or anxiety by creating a sense of urgency around sensitive topics like security breaches, identity theft, or financial losses. These tactics aim to create a false sense of urgency, encouraging victims to act quickly without fully thinking through the situation, which can increase the likelihood of them falling prey to the phishing attack.

Methodology

This experiment was conducted using the following methodology:

  1. Created a dataset of phishing-related information
  2. Split documents into chunks of 500 tokens with 50 token overlap
  3. Created embeddings using each model
  4. Built RAG systems using each model for both embeddings and generation
  5. Tested each system with the same set of cybersecurity queries
  6. Measured response time, relevance (compared to ground truth), and response length
